## Executive Summary

This document establishes the operational framework for delivering integrated Hepatitis C Virus (HCV) and Cardiovascular Disease (CVD) care through a **decentralized, patient-centric, task-sharing model** across the Rohingya refugee camps of Cox's Bazar, Bangladesh.

### Key Statistics & Context

- **Target Population**: ~884,000 Rohingya refugees across Camps 1W, 04, and 05
- **HCV Burden**: ~1 in 5 adults (~86,000 infected)
- **CVD Prevalence**: Estimated 15-20% in populations >40 years
- **CPI Implementation Areas**: Camp 1W (Blocks A, B, C) & Camp 04 (Blocks D, F)
- **Partner Implementation**: Camp 05 via RTMI (CPI HSS support)
- **Research Partner**: icddr,b (Care model design and evaluation)

### Model Innovation

Unlike centralized specialist-led systems that fail vulnerable populations, this model:

1. **Brings care to people** through CHW-led community screening
2. **Task-shifts** clinical roles to trained non-specialists
3. **Integrates** HCV and CVD with existing NCD services
4. **Enables same-day testing and treatment** to reduce loss-to-follow-up
5. **Prioritizes patient convenience** with single-location care
6. **Builds community trust** through stigma reduction and health education

## The Dual Crisis: HCV and CVD in Cox's Bazar

### HCV: A Silent Epidemic

The Rohingya refugee camps face a hidden public health emergency:

- **Scale**: An estimated 86,000 persons living with chronic HCV infection (17-20% of adults)
- **Silent nature**: 95% of HCV-positive individuals are unaware of their infection (often asymptomatic for 10-30 years)
- **Consequence if untreated**: Progression to cirrhosis (15-30% within 20 years), liver failure, hepatocellular carcinoma
- **Transmission routes**: Primarily unsafe medical procedures in pre-2015 Myanmar; current secondary transmission through limited access to sterile injections and unsafe blood products
- **Barrier to care**: 
  - No access to specialist liver services
  - Movement restrictions preventing camp departure
  - Complete lack of treatment availability prior to this program
  - Social stigma preventing disclosure

### CVD: An Accelerating Threat

Simultaneously, cardiovascular disease burden is rising:

- **Leading cause of death**: 67% of all mortality in Bangladesh; exacerbated in refugee settings
- **Risk factors amplified**: 
  - Prolonged stress and trauma exposure increase hypertension and CVD
  - Food insecurity and limited diet increase obesity and metabolic syndrome
  - Environmental factors (smoke from fires, pollution) worsen respiratory and cardiac disease
- **Comorbidity complexity**: 
  - High clustering of hypertension, diabetes, dyslipidemia, and obesity (cardiometabolic syndrome)
  - 56.8% of NCD clinic patients have >1 CVD risk factor
  - Insufficient lipid-lowering treatment despite high-risk profiles
- **Access barriers identical to HCV**:
  - Centralized services far from camps
  - Cost barriers
  - Language and health literacy barriers
  - Lack of integrated risk assessment

## Why Decentralization Works

### The Problem with Centralized Care

- Hospital-based HCV specialists are 50+ km away; impossible for refugee movement restrictions
- Multi-step care pathways (diagnosis → referral → confirmation → treatment) result in >40% loss-to-follow-up
- Waiting for specialist appointments (weeks to months) leads to patient disengagement
- Complicated eligibility assessments deter marginal patients from seeking help
- Costs of transportation and clinic visits prohibitive for refugees with zero income

### Evidence for Decentralization

- **Georgia**: Achieved 80% population screening and 80,000 treated (2022) through decentralized services
- **Rwanda**: Screened 7 million and treated 60,000 using rapid task-sharing to primary care
- **Nigeria**: Demonstrated feasibility of same-day RDT-to-treatment in primary health centers
- **WHO Recommendation**: Strongly recommends decentralization for HCV in low-resource settings; **95% cure rates maintained in primary care**
- **CVD Integration**: Cost-effective in decentralized models when integrated with NCD services

## Key Innovation Features

### 1. Decentralization
- 🏘️ Services delivered in communities, not distant hospitals
- 📍 Eliminates movement restrictions & travel barriers
- 🚶 Walking distance to care (same camp, same block)

### 2. Task-Shifting
- 👥 Non-specialists deliver 90%+ of care
- 💪 CHWs → screening & adherence support
- 🏥 Nurses/MAs → treatment management
- 👨‍⚕️ Physicians → complex cases only

### 3. Integration
- 🔗 HCV & CVD screened together (same visit)
- 📋 One registration, unified follow-up
- 💊 Simplified treatment protocols

### 4. Patient-Centricity
- ⏱️ Same-day testing and treatment
- 🏠 Services at familiar locations
- 💬 Community health workers as trusted advisors
