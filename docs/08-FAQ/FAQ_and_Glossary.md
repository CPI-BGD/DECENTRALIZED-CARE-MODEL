# Frequently Asked Questions & Glossary

**Version 3.0 | February 2026**

## Frequently Asked Questions

### About HCV

**Q: What is Hepatitis C?**  
A: Hepatitis C is a viral infection that affects the liver. The virus (HCV) can cause inflammation and damage to liver cells. Most people with acute HCV infection recover on their own, but about 15-20% develop chronic infection that persists for life.

**Q: How is HCV transmitted?**  
A: HCV is primarily transmitted through blood-to-blood contact. This can occur through unsafe medical procedures, sharing of contaminated needles, or rarely through sexual contact or mother-to-child transmission. HCV is NOT transmitted through food, water, sharing utensils, or casual contact.

**Q: What are the symptoms of HCV?**  
A: Many people with HCV have no symptoms and are unaware of their infection. When symptoms occur, they may include fatigue, abdominal pain, jaundice (yellowing of skin and eyes), dark urine, and pale stools. Symptoms typically appear 6-12 weeks after infection.

**Q: Can HCV be cured?**  
A: Yes! Modern direct-acting antiviral (DAA) medications cure HCV in 95%+ of cases. Treatment typically lasts 12 weeks and has minimal side effects. The cure is confirmed by testing for HCV RNA 12 weeks after treatment completion (SVR12).

**Q: What happens if HCV is not treated?**  
A: Without treatment, chronic HCV can progress to cirrhosis (liver scarring) over 15-30 years. Cirrhosis can lead to liver failure, hepatocellular carcinoma (liver cancer), and death. Treatment prevents these complications.

### About the Program

**Q: Who is eligible for HCV screening?**  
A: All adults 18 years and older in the target camps are eligible for screening. Special outreach is provided to adults 40+ years who have higher HCV prevalence. Screening is free and confidential.

**Q: Where can I get screened for HCV?**  
A: Screening is available at community health posts, clinics, and community screening events. Community health workers can provide information on screening locations and times.

**Q: What is the screening test?**  
A: The screening test is a rapid diagnostic test (RDT) that detects HCV antibodies in blood. The test takes about 15 minutes and results are provided immediately. If positive, confirmatory testing is arranged.

**Q: Is the screening test painful?**  
A: The test involves a small finger prick to obtain a blood sample. This is minimally painful and the discomfort is brief. The needle used is sterile and discarded after use.

**Q: What does a positive screening test mean?**  
A: A positive screening test means HCV antibodies were detected. This indicates either current or past HCV infection. Confirmatory testing is needed to determine if the infection is active (chronic) or resolved.

**Q: Is HCV treatment free?**  
A: Yes, HCV treatment is completely free for all eligible patients. There are no costs for medications, clinic visits, or laboratory tests. All services are provided by the CPI-CARE program.

**Q: How long is HCV treatment?**  
A: Standard HCV treatment is 12 weeks. Patients take one tablet daily for 12 weeks. After treatment, follow-up testing is done at 12 weeks (SVR12) to confirm cure.

**Q: What are the side effects of HCV treatment?**  
A: Most patients tolerate HCV treatment well. Common mild side effects include fatigue, headache, nausea, and diarrhea. These side effects usually improve over time and can be managed with simple remedies. Serious side effects are rare.

### About CVD

**Q: What is cardiovascular disease (CVD)?**  
A: CVD includes diseases of the heart and blood vessels such as high blood pressure, heart attack, and stroke. Risk factors include hypertension, diabetes, high cholesterol, obesity, smoking, and physical inactivity.

**Q: Why is CVD screening important?**  
A: Many people have CVD risk factors without knowing it. Early detection and treatment of risk factors can prevent heart attacks and strokes. The program screens for CVD risk factors during HCV screening.

**Q: What is high blood pressure?**  
A: High blood pressure (hypertension) occurs when the force of blood against artery walls is too high. Normal BP is <120/80 mmHg. Hypertension is BP ≥140/90 mmHg. High BP can damage the heart and kidneys if not treated.

**Q: Can high blood pressure be treated?**  
A: Yes, high blood pressure can be effectively treated with medications and lifestyle changes. Treatment reduces the risk of heart attack and stroke. Free medications are available through the program.

**Q: What is diabetes?**  
A: Diabetes is a condition where the body cannot properly regulate blood sugar levels. Type 2 diabetes (the most common type) occurs when the body becomes resistant to insulin. Diabetes can lead to complications including heart disease, kidney disease, and blindness if not treated.

**Q: Can diabetes be prevented or treated?**  
A: Type 2 diabetes can often be prevented or delayed through weight loss, physical activity, and healthy diet. If diabetes develops, it can be effectively managed with medications and lifestyle changes. Free screening and treatment are available through the program.

### About Partnerships

**Q: What is CPI's role in the program?**  
A: CPI is the core implementer of the program in Camps 1W and 04. CPI provides overall program coordination, staff training, supervision, and quality assurance. CPI also provides health systems strengthening support to RTMI in Camp 05.

**Q: What is RTMI's role?**  
A: RTMI is the implementing partner in Camp 05. RTMI directly implements screening and treatment services in Camp 05 with technical support and capacity building from CPI.

**Q: What is icddr,b's role?**  
A: icddr,b is the research partner. icddr,b conducts the randomized controlled trial to evaluate the care model, provides technical support on protocols, and generates evidence for program improvement and scale-up.

**Q: What is WHO's role?**  
A: WHO provides technical support including guidelines, training materials, supervision, and quality assurance. WHO also provides advocacy support for policy integration and scale-up.

## Glossary of Terms

**APRI Score**: A non-invasive test to assess liver fibrosis using AST and platelet count. Scores <0.5 indicate minimal fibrosis; >1.5 indicate significant fibrosis.

**Chronic HCV Infection**: HCV infection that persists for more than 6 months. Chronic infection can lead to cirrhosis and liver cancer if untreated.

**CVD**: Cardiovascular disease; diseases of the heart and blood vessels including hypertension, heart attack, and stroke.

**DAA**: Direct-acting antiviral; medications that directly target the HCV virus and cure HCV infection in 95%+ of cases.

**Decompensated Cirrhosis**: Advanced liver disease with complications such as ascites, variceal bleeding, or hepatic encephalopathy. Patients with decompensated cirrhosis require specialist care.

**Dyslipidemia**: Abnormal blood lipid levels including high cholesterol or triglycerides, or low HDL cholesterol. Dyslipidemia is a risk factor for CVD.

**eGFR**: Estimated glomerular filtration rate; a measure of kidney function. eGFR <30 indicates severe kidney disease.

**Fibrosis**: Scarring of the liver caused by chronic inflammation. Fibrosis is staged F0-F4, with F0 being no fibrosis and F4 being cirrhosis.

**Genotype**: The genetic variant of HCV. There are 6 main genotypes. Different genotypes may require different treatment regimens.

**HCV**: Hepatitis C virus; the virus that causes hepatitis C infection.

**HCV RNA**: The genetic material of the HCV virus. Presence of HCV RNA indicates active viral replication and chronic infection. Absence of HCV RNA indicates cure.

**Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC)**: Liver cancer that can develop in patients with cirrhosis. HCC is a leading cause of cancer death worldwide.

**Hypertension**: High blood pressure; systolic BP ≥140 mmHg or diastolic BP ≥90 mmHg. Hypertension is a major risk factor for heart disease and stroke.

**Loss-to-Follow-Up (LTFU)**: Patients who do not return for scheduled appointments or treatment. High LTFU rates reduce treatment effectiveness.

**M&E**: Monitoring and evaluation; the system for tracking program progress and assessing outcomes.

**NCD**: Non-communicable disease; chronic diseases such as HCV, CVD, diabetes, and cancer.

**RCT**: Randomized controlled trial; a research study that randomly assigns participants to different treatment groups to compare effectiveness.

**RDT**: Rapid diagnostic test; a quick test that detects HCV antibodies in blood with results in 15 minutes.

**SVR**: Sustained virological response; absence of HCV RNA at 12 weeks (SVR12) or 24 weeks (SVR24) after treatment completion. SVR indicates cure.

**Task-Shifting**: Delegation of clinical tasks from specialists to non-specialists such as nurses or community health workers. Task-shifting improves access to care and reduces costs.

**Viral Load**: The amount of HCV RNA in the blood. High viral load indicates active infection; undetectable viral load indicates cure.
