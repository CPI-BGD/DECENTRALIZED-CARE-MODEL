# CVD Risk Assessment & Management Protocol

**Version 3.0 | February 2026**

## Overview

This protocol provides comprehensive guidance for cardiovascular disease risk assessment and management in the decentralized care model, integrated with HCV screening and treatment services.

## CVD Risk Factors

### Primary Risk Factors

The major cardiovascular risk factors assessed include hypertension (blood pressure ≥140/90 mmHg), diabetes mellitus (fasting glucose ≥126 mg/dL or on treatment), dyslipidemia (total cholesterol >200 mg/dL or on treatment), obesity (BMI ≥30 kg/m²), and smoking status. Additional risk factors include physical inactivity, unhealthy diet, excessive alcohol use, and family history of premature CVD.

### Comorbidity Assessment

Many patients have multiple CVD risk factors simultaneously. Approximately 56.8% of NCD clinic patients have more than one CVD risk factor. The presence of multiple risk factors significantly increases cardiovascular event risk and requires more intensive management.

## Screening Procedure

### Blood Pressure Measurement

Blood pressure should be measured using a calibrated sphygmomanometer or automated device. Take measurements in a sitting position after 5 minutes of rest. Record both systolic and diastolic readings. If the first reading is elevated, repeat after 5 minutes and record both values.

### Anthropometric Measurements

Measure height and weight to calculate BMI (weight in kg divided by height in meters squared). Measure waist circumference at the midpoint between the lower rib margin and iliac crest. Record all measurements in the patient's file.

### Laboratory Tests

Obtain fasting blood glucose, total cholesterol, HDL cholesterol, LDL cholesterol, and triglycerides. For patients with diabetes or on treatment, obtain HbA1c. Perform urinalysis to screen for proteinuria. These tests should be performed at baseline and annually for monitoring.

## Risk Stratification

### Low Risk
Patients with no CVD risk factors or only one risk factor (such as hypertension alone) are classified as low risk. These patients require lifestyle modification counseling and annual monitoring.

### Moderate Risk
Patients with 2-3 CVD risk factors or those with one major risk factor (such as diabetes) are classified as moderate risk. These patients require pharmacological treatment for identified risk factors and close monitoring every 3-6 months.

### High Risk
Patients with 4 or more risk factors, established CVD, or those with diabetes and additional risk factors are classified as high risk. These patients require intensive pharmacological treatment and close monitoring every 1-3 months.

## Management Protocols

### Hypertension Management

**Stage 1 Hypertension (140-159/90-99 mmHg)**: Initiate lifestyle modifications including dietary sodium reduction, weight loss, physical activity, and stress management. After 3 months, if BP remains elevated, start antihypertensive medication.

**Stage 2 Hypertension (≥160/≥100 mmHg)**: Start antihypertensive medication immediately along with lifestyle modifications. First-line agents include ACE inhibitors, calcium channel blockers, or thiazide diuretics.

**Target BP**: <140/90 mmHg for most patients; <130/80 mmHg for those with diabetes or chronic kidney disease.

### Diabetes Management

**Newly Diagnosed Diabetes**: Initiate lifestyle modifications including dietary changes, weight loss, and physical activity. Start metformin 500 mg daily (if tolerated) and titrate to 1000-1500 mg daily. Monitor fasting glucose and HbA1c every 3 months.

**Established Diabetes**: Continue metformin and add additional agents if needed to achieve HbA1c <7%. Consider adding sulfonylureas, DPP-4 inhibitors, or GLP-1 agonists based on patient characteristics and comorbidities.

**Target HbA1c**: <7% for most patients; <6.5% for those with short disease duration and no complications; <8% for elderly patients or those with multiple comorbidities.

### Dyslipidemia Management

**Lipid Targets**: LDL cholesterol <100 mg/dL for most patients; <70 mg/dL for high-risk patients. HDL cholesterol >40 mg/dL for men and >50 mg/dL for women. Triglycerides <150 mg/dL.

**First-Line Treatment**: Statins are the first-line agents for dyslipidemia. Start atorvastatin 20 mg daily or simvastatin 20 mg daily. Titrate dose based on lipid response and tolerability.

**Additional Agents**: If statins are insufficient, add ezetimibe 10 mg daily or consider bempedoic acid for additional LDL reduction.

## Lifestyle Modification Counseling

### Dietary Recommendations

Recommend a diet rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and lean proteins. Reduce sodium intake to <2300 mg daily (preferably <1500 mg daily). Limit saturated fat to <7% of total calories and eliminate trans fats. Reduce added sugar and sugary beverages. Encourage home cooking and limit processed foods.

### Physical Activity

Recommend at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week or 75 minutes of vigorous-intensity activity. Include resistance training 2-3 times per week. Encourage daily activities such as walking, climbing stairs, or gardening.

### Weight Management

For overweight or obese patients, recommend gradual weight loss of 5-10% of body weight over 6 months. Provide practical strategies for dietary modification and increased physical activity. Consider referral to nutritionist if available.

### Smoking Cessation

Strongly advise all smokers to quit. Provide counseling and consider pharmacological support (nicotine replacement therapy or varenicline) if available. Refer to smoking cessation programs if available.

## Follow-up and Monitoring

### Initial Follow-up

Schedule follow-up appointments 2-4 weeks after initial assessment to review lifestyle modifications and assess medication tolerability. Repeat BP measurements and laboratory tests as indicated.

### Ongoing Monitoring

For stable patients on treatment, schedule follow-up every 3-6 months. For newly diagnosed or unstable patients, schedule follow-up every 1-3 months. Repeat laboratory tests annually or more frequently if indicated by clinical status.

### Complication Screening

Screen annually for diabetic complications including retinopathy, nephropathy, and neuropathy. Screen for signs of heart failure, stroke, or peripheral vascular disease. Refer to specialists as needed for complications.
