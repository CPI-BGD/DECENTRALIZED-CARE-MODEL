# HCV Treatment Management Protocol

**Version 3.0 | February 2026**

## Treatment Eligibility

### Inclusion Criteria

Patients eligible for HCV treatment include those with confirmed HCV RNA positive status (chronic infection), age 18 years or older, no contraindications to direct-acting antivirals (DAAs), and demonstrated willingness to adhere to the treatment regimen. All eligible patients should be offered treatment regardless of fibrosis stage.

### Exclusion Criteria

Patients should not receive DAA treatment if they have severe liver disease (Child-Pugh C classification), decompensated cirrhosis, active substance abuse, pregnancy or breastfeeding status, or severe renal impairment (eGFR <30 mL/min). These conditions require specialist evaluation and modified treatment approaches.

## Treatment Regimens

### Genotype 1 Treatment

For patients with HCV genotype 1, the recommended regimen is Sofosbuvir/Ledipasvir (400/90 mg daily) for 12 weeks. This combination achieves cure rates of 95-98% and requires monitoring at baseline, week 4, week 12, and SVR12 (12 weeks post-treatment).

### Genotype 3 Treatment

For patients with HCV genotype 3, the recommended regimen is Sofosbuvir/Velpatasvir (400/100 mg daily) for 12 weeks. This combination achieves cure rates of 95-98% with the same monitoring schedule as genotype 1.

### Genotype 6 Treatment

For patients with HCV genotype 6, the recommended regimen is Sofosbuvir/Velpatasvir (400/100 mg daily) for 12 weeks. This combination achieves cure rates of 95-98% and requires the same monitoring protocol.

## Treatment Monitoring

### Baseline Assessment

Before starting treatment, conduct a comprehensive baseline assessment including HCV RNA level, liver function tests (ALT, AST, albumin, bilirubin), kidney function (creatinine, eGFR), platelet count, and screening for potential drug interactions. This ensures patient safety and identifies any contraindications.

### During Treatment Monitoring

At week 4 of treatment, conduct a clinical assessment and check patient adherence to the regimen. Repeat this assessment at week 8. At week 12 (end of treatment), perform a final clinical assessment and HCV RNA test to confirm viral clearance.

### Post-Treatment Follow-up

At 12 weeks post-treatment (SVR12), perform HCV RNA testing to confirm sustained virological response. If indicated, repeat testing at 24 weeks post-treatment (SVR24). For patients with cirrhosis, conduct annual liver function monitoring to detect any complications.

## Adverse Event Management

### Common Side Effects

Common side effects during DAA treatment include fatigue (usually mild and improves over time), headache (manage with paracetamol), nausea (manage with ginger and small frequent meals), and diarrhea (manage with dietary modifications). These side effects are generally mild and do not require treatment discontinuation.

### Serious Adverse Events

Serious adverse events requiring immediate intervention include severe hepatotoxicity (stop treatment and hospitalize), severe renal impairment (adjust dosing or stop treatment), and severe drug interactions (adjust concomitant medications). Any serious adverse event requires medical evaluation and possible specialist consultation.

## Treatment Adherence Support

### Patient Education

Provide clear written and verbal instructions on medication taking, including timing, food requirements, and potential interactions. Explain the importance of completing the full 12-week course even if the patient feels well. Discuss expected side effects and when to seek help.

### Adherence Monitoring

Use multiple strategies to support adherence including pill counts at each visit, patient-held treatment cards, SMS reminders, and peer support groups. Identify and address barriers to adherence such as transportation, work schedules, or side effects.

### Loss-to-Follow-Up Prevention

Maintain contact with patients through home visits if needed, provide flexible clinic schedules, and use community health workers for follow-up support. Address social determinants that may affect adherence such as food insecurity or housing instability.
